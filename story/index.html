Sex Differences in Daily Rhythms

Comparing male and female mouse temperature and activity patterns over 14 days, revealing how biological sex and the estrus cycle influence circadian rhythms and physiological regulation.

Day 1
Day 1: Establishing Sex Differences

Day 1: Establishing Sex Differences

We begin our 14-day observation comparing male (blue) and female (red) mice. Notice that female mice consistently maintain higher body temperatures throughout the day, typically 0.2-0.3°C warmer than males. Both sexes show peak activity during the dark phase, though females demonstrate slightly higher activity levels at the upper ranges.

Day 2: Consistent Sex-Based Patterns

The sex-based differences remain stable on Day 2. Female mice continue to run warmer than males across all time points. The activity patterns are remarkably consistent with Day 1, establishing our baseline for detecting hormonal influences in the coming days.

Day 3: Baseline Established

By Day 3, the consistent temperature differential between sexes is clear. Female body temperature remains elevated compared to males, particularly during peak activity hours. These three days of baseline data will help us identify estrus-related changes.

Day 4: Pre-Estrus Stability

Day 4 shows continued stable patterns. The temperature gap between males and females persists, with females maintaining their characteristic elevation. Activity distributions remain consistent, with females showing that slight edge at higher activity levels.

Day 5: Approaching First Estrus

On Day 5, subtle changes begin to emerge in the female data. While the overall temperature remains elevated compared to males, we start to see slightly increased variability in female temperature patterns, suggesting approaching hormonal changes.

Day 6: First Estrus Peak

Day 6 marks the first estrus phase. Female body temperature shows a notable rise, creating an even larger gap compared to male temperatures. The elevation is most pronounced during the dark phase, where female temperatures can exceed male temperatures by up to 0.5°C. Activity levels also show increased intensity in females.

Day 7: Post-Estrus Recovery

Following the estrus peak, Day 7 shows females gradually returning toward their baseline temperature elevation. However, they still maintain higher temperatures than males. The activity patterns begin to normalize, though females continue to show that characteristic higher range activity.

Day 8: Inter-Estrus Period

Day 8 represents the inter-estrus period. Female temperatures remain consistently higher than males but have stabilized from the Day 6 peak. This pattern demonstrates the cyclic nature of the estrus effect overlaid on the baseline sex difference.

Day 9: Preparing for Next Cycle

On Day 9, we observe stable patterns with females maintaining their typical temperature elevation over males. The system appears to be preparing for the next estrus phase, with subtle increases in temperature variability appearing in the female data.

Day 10: Second Estrus Peak

Day 10 brings the second estrus phase. Once again, female body temperatures rise significantly above their already-elevated baseline, creating a pronounced difference from male temperatures. The activity distribution also shows increased female activity at the high end, demonstrating the behavioral component of estrus.

Day 11: Gradual Decline

Following the second estrus peak, Day 11 shows a gradual decline in female temperatures, though they remain substantially higher than male temperatures. The activity patterns also begin to normalize, returning toward the baseline sex-based differences.

Day 12: Stable Inter-Estrus

Day 12 represents another stable inter-estrus period. The consistent temperature differential between sexes is maintained, with females running warmer throughout the circadian cycle. Activity patterns show the typical slight female advantage at higher ranges.

Day 13: Pre-Estrus Indicators

As we approach the final estrus phase of our observation period, Day 13 shows subtle pre-estrus indicators. Female temperature variability begins to increase, and the baseline temperature elevation becomes more pronounced, particularly during peak activity hours.

Day 14: Third Estrus Peak

Day 14 concludes our observation with the third estrus phase. Female temperatures once again surge above their baseline, creating the largest temperature differential from males. This regular 4-day cycle demonstrates how estrus effects compound with baseline sex differences, resulting in significant physiological variations that must be considered in biomedical research.
