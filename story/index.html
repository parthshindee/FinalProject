Day 1: Establishing Baseline

We begin with the first day of observation. Notice how female mice's activity peaks during the dark phase, while core temperature follows a parallel—but slightly offset—rhythm. This baseline is crucial for comparing later when estrus effects emerge.

Day 2: Pattern Consistency

On Day 2, the circadian rhythms remain remarkably stable. Activity and temperature curves overlay nearly identically onto yesterday's. The mouse's internal clock has “locked in” its daily rhythm, giving us a strong reference point.

Day 3: Reinforced Rhythms

By Day 3, the female mouse's circadian signature is well-established. Minor hour-to-hour fluctuations are normal, but overall the curves maintain a consistent shape. These three days of baseline data form the foundation for spotting any estrus-related shifts.

Day 4: Early Estrus Signals

On Day 4, subtle deviations appear. Activity peaks grow slightly higher, and core temperature shows an extra bump in the early evening. These early signals indicate that hormonal changes are beginning to influence daily physiology.

Day 5: Peak Estrus

Day 5 marks the peak of estrus-driven modulation. Activity levels can jump well above baseline during the dark phase, and temperature fluctuations become more pronounced. This dramatic shift demonstrates how the estrus cycle can strongly modulate both behavior and physiology.

Day 6: Estrus Continues

The effects persist on Day 6, though usually begin to taper from the Day 5 peak. Elevated activity and more variable temperature curves remain. By tracking this entire period, we see the full duration of estrus effects on daily rhythms.

Day 7: Recovery Phase

As Day 7 arrives, patterns gradually return toward baseline. Activity and temperature are still slightly elevated relative to Days 1-3, but the biggest spikes have subsided. This “recovery” highlights the resilience of the circadian system: it takes time for daily rhythms to stabilize after hormonal surges.
